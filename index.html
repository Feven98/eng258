Overview

Harlem Renaissance was a significant cultural and artistic movement that emerged in the early 20th century, particularly during the 1920s and 1930s, centered in the Harlem neighborhood of New York City. This period marked a pivotal moment in African American history and literature, characterized by a surge of creativity, intellectualism, and cultural expression among Black artists, writers, musicians, and thinkers.

- There are three defining characteristics that makes Harlem Renaissance different from the others period.

A. Great Migration of African Americans from rural to urban spaces and from South to North

It was a significant movement of African Americans from the rural South to urban areas in the North of the United States during the early 1900s to 1970s. Many Black people left the South to escape racism, poverty, and limited opportunities, seeking better jobs and a chance for a better life in Northern cities. This migration led to major changes in demographics, as cities like New York, Chicago, and Detroit saw an increase in Black populations. It also brought about a cultural renaissance known as the Harlem Renaissance, with Black artists and writers flourishing in urban centers. Despite facing discrimination and challenges in their new homes, the Great Migration had a lasting impact on African American culture, civil rights, and urban life in America.

B. Artistic Expression and Creativity

It encompass the diverse ways individuals or communities use art forms like painting, sculpture, literature, music, dance, and theater to communicate emotions, ideas, and experiences. Artists employ originality and skill to convey personal perspectives or cultural themes, contributing to cultural innovation and enriching society with unique artistic expressions. Through creativity, artists explore new techniques, challenge conventions, and address important issues, connecting with audiences on emotional and intellectual levels while expressing their identities and perspectives.

C. Cultural Empowerment and Economic Opportunity

It refers to efforts to strengthen African American culture and promote pride while also creating better jobs and economic opportunities. During movements like the Harlem Renaissance, artists and writers celebrated Black culture to combat stereotypes and empower the community. At the same time, initiatives like the Great Migration aimed to improve job prospects and living conditions for African Americans in Northern cities. Despite challenges, these efforts sought to provide pathways to economic success through education and civil rights activism. Together, these initiatives fostered empowerment and progress within the African American community.

Harlem Renaissance Authors

The Harlem Renaissance was an exsiting period that saw the rise of numerous influential African American writers. Many writers emerged, each making a significant impact on culture and literature. Here are four notable authors from that time:

1. Claude McKay(1889-1948)

  • Claude McKay was a prominent writer during the Harlem Renaissance known for his poetry and novels. He was born in Jamaica and later moved to the United States, where he became a key figure in the African American literary scene.
  • McKay's work often addressed themes of racial identity, social injustice, and the experiences of Black people in America. His poetry captured the struggles and aspirations of African Americans, advocating for dignity, equality, and empowerment
  • For example, one of his most famous poems "If We Must Die” has a line that urges resilience and resistance in the face of adversity:

    "If we must die, let it not be like hogs

    Hunted and penned in an inglorious spot,

    While round us bark the mad and hungry dogs,

    Making their mock at our accursed lot."

    • 2. Zora Neale Hurston(1891-1960)

  • Zora Neale Hurston was a renowned writer of the Harlem Renaissance known for her captivating novels and studies of African American folklore. Her most famous work, "Their Eyes Were Watching God," follows the journey of Janie Crawford as she seeks love and independence in the context of Southern Black culture. Hurston's writing is characterized by vibrant storytelling and a deep appreciation for Black traditions and resilience. Beyond her novels, she collected and documented African American folklore, preserving cultural heritage through work like "Mules and Men".
  • For example, in her novel "Their Eyes Were Watching God", there is a line that tells that life unfolds in stages, with some periods marked by uncertainty and searching and others by clarity and resolution. It goes like this:

    • “There are years that ask questions and years that answer."

    3. Langston Hughes(1902-1967)

  • Langston Hughes, a prominent figure of the Harlem Renaissance, was known for his powerful poetry that celebrated the experiences and struggles of African Americans.
  • He emerged as one of the movement's most influential poets, known for his innovative use of jazz rhythms and African American vernacular in his poetry. He captured the joys and struggles of Black life in America, addressing themes of identity, oppression, and resilience.
  • For example, in his poem "Harlem", the line showen below shows the consequences of delaying or denying one's dreams and aspirations.

    • "What happens to a dream deferred? Does it dry up like a raisin in the sun?"

    4. Countee Cullen(1903-1946)

  • Countee Cullen was a renowned poet of the Harlem Renaissance known for his elegant style and exploration of race and identity. Adopted and raised in Harlem, Cullen excelled academically and began writing poetry in his youth. His work is characterized by lyrical beauty and emotional depth, making him a significant figure in American literature.
  • For example, from his famous poem "Heritage", there is a line that reflects on the loss of African roots among Black Americans:

    • "I asked the heaven of stars

    What I should give my love

    It answered me with silence,

    Silence above."

    Important social events during Harlem Renaissance

    The Harlem Renaissance marked by significant events that influenced and intersected with the literary and artistic movements of the time. Here are some of the timeline highlighting important social events and achievements:

  • In 1909, The National Association for the Advancement of Colored People (NAACP) was founded by an interracial group including W.E.B. Du Bois, Ida B. Wells-Barnett, and Mary White Ovington.
  • In 1910, The first issue of The Crisis, an American quarterly magazine published by the NAACP, is released.
  • In 1916, The Great Migration, a widespread migration of African Americans from the rural South to the urban North and West, begins about this time.
  • In 1918, Black nationalist Marcus Garvey begins publishing Negro World, a newspaper promoting African culture.
  • In 1919, The Red Summer occurs, marked by race riots and violent attacks against Black communities in cities across the United States, highlighting racial tensions and discrimination.
  • In 1919, Poet Claude McKay publishes his famous poem “If We Must Die” and emerges as the first and most militant voice of the movement's writers.
  • In 1921, Shuffle Along, a musical by Eubie Blake and Noble Sissle, opens on Broadway and makes a major contribution to the Harlem Renaissance, most significantly by opening the way for a number of other Black shows that laid a foundation for the “Jazz Age” of the 1920s.
  • In 1923, Charles Spurgeon Johnson, a leader in race relations, starts Opportunity magazine. Toomer publishes his experimental novel Cane, about the African American experience.
  • In 1924, Louis Armstrong emerges as the first great jazz soloist when he moves from King Oliver’s Creole Jazz Band in Chicago to Fletcher Henderson’s band in New York City.
  • In 1925, Alain Locke's anthology of Black writers, The New Negro, is published.
  • In 1926, Langston Hughes’s poetry collection The Weary Blues is published by Alfred A. Knopf.
  • In 1927, James Weldon Johnson publishes God’s Trombones, a collection of black dialect sermons in poetic form.
  • In 1928, Poet Claude McKay publishes his first novel Home to Harlem, which is said to be the most widely read novel written by an African American up to that time.
  • In 1937, Writer Zora Neale Hurston publishes her second novel, Their Eyes Were Watching God.

    • In conclusion, the Harlem Renaissance stands as a tribute to the strength, ingenuity, and cultural influence of African Americans. It underscores the significance of artistic expression and cultural heritage in driving societal change and promoting mutual understanding among diverse communities. The enduring impact of this period resonates today, fueling ongoing initiatives for equality, inclusivity, and the recognition of diverse perspectives within American culture.
